The Manifesto

Academic Dissent exists for a simple reason: too much published research is treated as authoritative long before it has earned that authority.

In principle, research should be a public test of ideas. A paper makes a claim, exposes its methods, presents its evidence, and invites scrutiny. Others examine the assumptions, challenge the identification, test the robustness, replicate the results, and, where necessary, reject the conclusion. That is how knowledge advances. Science is not a ceremony of publication. It is a discipline of organised doubt.

Yet the culture surrounding research increasingly rewards affirmation over criticism, branding over substance, and alignment over truth. Publication is often mistaken for validation. Citation is confused with proof. Prestige is allowed to stand in for rigour. Entire debates are sometimes settled socially before they are settled scientifically.

This problem is especially acute for those who work within institutions, and above all within public institutions. Many researchers, analysts, economists, and subject-matter experts operate under formal and informal constraints that make frank criticism difficult. Official communication rules, reputational concerns, managerial sensitivities, legal caution, and career incentives all push in the same direction: speak carefully, speak moderately, and above all do not publicly challenge the wrong consensus in the wrong way. Even when criticism is methodologically sound, professionally relevant, and in the public interest, it is often softened, delayed, internalised, or never voiced at all.

The result is not merely personal frustration. It is an epistemic failure. When institutions cannot easily accommodate open internal dissent, errors survive longer than they should. Weak papers continue to circulate. Fragile empirical claims become embedded in slide decks, policy notes, consultation papers, conference remarks, and public narratives. A flawed result that should have been corrected in an early seminar can end up shaping decisions with real consequences.

This matters because research does not remain inside journals. It travels. It informs regulation, legislation, market design, supervisory practice, public spending, and institutional strategy. When weak evidence is dressed up as settled science, the costs are not abstract. Public funds are wasted. Resources are diverted toward fashionable but unsupported ideas. Problems are misdiagnosed. Trade-offs are obscured. Bad policies are defended with the language of objectivity even when the underlying evidence is partial, selective, or unsound.

The ideal of evidence-based policymaking is therefore under pressure. Too often, it is quietly replaced by policy-based evidence making: a process in which a preferred conclusion appears first, and evidence is then selected, framed, or exaggerated to support it.

Research becomes less a tool of discovery than a tool of justification. Assumptions are chosen because they are convenient. Results are highlighted because they are useful. Uncertainty is minimised because hesitation is institutionally inconvenient. What should be an honest encounter with reality becomes an exercise in retrospective legitimation.

Academic Dissent rejects that drift.

We begin from a plain conviction: no claim should be protected from scrutiny by the status of its author, the prestige of its journal, the urgency of its political use, or the institutional comfort it provides. If a result is robust, it will survive criticism. If it cannot survive criticism, it does not deserve authority.

This site exists to create space for what many institutions struggle to permit openly: frank, rigorous, methodical criticism of published work. Its purpose is not cynicism, contrarianism, or performance. It is not a stage for outrage and not a vehicle for personal attacks. It is a forum for disciplined dissent. We criticise arguments, not persons; methods, not motives; evidence, not identity. We assume good faith where possible, but we do not confuse good faith with correctness.

Our Core Principles

1. Rigour before reputation. The identity, title, affiliation, and status of an author are irrelevant to the validity of a claim. A weak argument does not become strong because it appears in a prestigious venue. A sound objection does not become weak because it comes from an obscure or anonymous source.

2. Evidence before narrative. We are not interested in whether a conclusion is fashionable, convenient, or institutionally useful. We are interested in whether it is supported. Where the data do not sustain the claim, the claim must yield.

3. Transparency before assertion. Wherever possible, criticism should show its workings: assumptions stated clearly, formulas rendered explicitly, code exposed, data treatment explained, alternative specifications presented. Mere suspicion is not enough. Dissent should be demonstrable.

4. Proportionality before polemic. The aim is not to maximise denunciation. It is to calibrate judgment. Some papers deserve caution; some deserve correction; some deserve rejection. The response should fit the defect.

5. Public reason before institutional convenience. Research that enters public debate, public administration, or public policy acquires a public burden. It must be answerable not only to peers, but to reasoned scrutiny. Where public money, public authority, or public credibility are involved, the standard should be higher, not lower.

The Necessity of Anonymity

We defend the legitimacy of anonymous criticism when anonymity is the condition of honesty. In an ideal world, arguments would always be made openly, under one’s own name, without fear of retaliation or institutional consequence. In the world as it exists, that condition often does not hold. People inside firms, universities, ministries, central banks, regulators, think tanks, and international organisations may see weaknesses that they cannot safely challenge in public.

Anonymity is sometimes dismissed as cowardice. In reality, it can be a necessary shield for truthful speech where formal independence is limited and informal pressures are strong. What matters is not whether criticism is signed, but whether it is rigorous. Academic Dissent does not ask readers to trust its tone. It asks them to inspect its reasoning.

Conclusion

The ambition of this project is modest in form and radical in implication. It does not seek to replace journals, universities, or policy institutions. It seeks to remind them of their own standards. It seeks to keep alive the idea that criticism is not disloyalty, that dissent is not obstruction, and that the correction of error is a public good.

A healthy research culture should welcome the sentence: “This result does not hold.”
A healthy policy culture should welcome the sentence: “The evidence is not sufficient.”
A healthy institution should welcome the sentence: “We may be wrong.”

When those sentences become difficult to say, error becomes easier to preserve. Academic Dissent exists to make them sayable again. Its mission is to support a frank and rigorous scientific debate grounded in rigour and evidence.

Where evidence is weak, we will say so.
Where methods fail, we will show how.
Where conclusions outrun results, we will narrow them.
Where policy seeks cover in bad research, we will withdraw that cover.

Not because dissent is fashionable, but because truth requires opposition.
