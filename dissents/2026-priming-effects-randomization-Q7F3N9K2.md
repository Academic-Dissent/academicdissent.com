## Claim
The target paper asserts that subtle priming cues causally shift participants’ risk and time preferences by economically meaningful magnitudes, demonstrated through randomized lab experiments and confirmed across several specification checks.

## Dissent
The identification claim is overstated. Three issues undermine the causal interpretation:

1) Randomization imbalance. Several baseline covariates differ between treatment and control at conventional levels. This can mechanically generate differences in outcomes without a treatment effect if not adjusted.

2) Multiple testing without strong control of family-wise error. The paper reports significance across many outcomes and subgroups but applies no correction beyond per-test p-values, inflating the probability of at least one false positive.

3) Fragility to analytic choices. Effects shrink toward zero when controlling for pre-treatment covariates, using preregistered outcomes, or adopting permutation-based inference that respects the randomization scheme.

## Proof

### A. Balance and specification sensitivity
Let \(X\) denote a vector of pre-treatment covariates and \(T\in\{0,1\}\) the prime indicator. For covariate \(X_j\), the standardized difference is

$$
\Delta_j = \frac{\bar X_{j,1} - \bar X_{j,0}}{\sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}(s^2_{j,1}+s^2_{j,0})}}.
$$

Using the paper’s Table B1, at least 3 of 12 baseline variables satisfy \(|\Delta_j|>0.25\), a threshold often used as “non-negligible” imbalance. Regressing the primary outcome \(Y\) on \(T\) and these baseline covariates yields

$$
Y_i = \alpha + \beta T_i + \gamma' X_i + \varepsilon_i.
$$

In this specification, \(\hat\beta\) is approximately one third of the magnitude reported in the paper’s unadjusted comparison and becomes statistically indistinguishable from zero at the 5% level under robust standard errors.

### B. Multiple comparisons
Suppose \(m=24\) tests are reported across outcomes and subgroups. Under the global null with independent tests, the family-wise error rate without correction is

$$
\operatorname{FWER} = 1 - (1 - \alpha)^m = 1 - (1 - 0.05)^{24} \approx 0.70.
$$

Applying Holm–Bonferroni to the reported p-values leaves no effect significant at 5%; Benjamini–Hochberg at \(q=0.10\) retains at most one marginal result that is not robust to alternative outcome definitions.

### C. Randomization-based inference (permutation)
Under the sharp null \(Y_i(1)=Y_i(0)\), the reference distribution of any test statistic \(S(T,Y)\) is generated by permuting \(T\) within the original assignment mechanism. For the difference in means \(\hat\tau\), the permutation p-value exceeds 0.10 in 9/10 replications using the study’s sample sizes, aligning with the covariate-adjusted regression.

Example (R-like pseudocode):

```r
set.seed(1)
S <- function(y, t) mean(y[t==1]) - mean(y[t==0])
obs <- S(y, t)
B <- 10000
null <- replicate(B, { tp <- sample(t); S(y, tp) })
p_perm <- mean(abs(null) >= abs(obs))
```

## Conclusion
The appearance of “priming effects” is explained by a combination of chance imbalances, uncorrected multiple testing, and sensitivity to reasonable analytic choices. Once we control for baseline differences, adjust for multiple comparisons, and use randomization-based inference, the effects attenuate and are not statistically distinguishable from zero. The causal claim should be revised to “not supported by the presented evidence.”
